Every ceramic coating manufacturer includes a surface prep step in their application instructions. Most owners read past it. That decision is why coatings fail, delaminate early, or cure with uneven gloss and water behavior that looks nothing like the product's advertised performance.

The panel wipe is not optional. It is the step that determines whether the coating bonds to bare, prepared paint – or bonds to whatever invisible residue was sitting on top of it.

## What the Panel Wipe Is Removing

After a wash, clay bar, and paint correction sequence, the paint surface appears clean. It is not clean in the way ceramic coating chemistry requires.

Polish residue is the primary problem. Any compound or polish used during paint correction leaves behind lubricant oils, glaze fillers, and carrier solvents that do not rinse away with water. They are designed to stay on the surface – that is what gives post-correction paint its immediate depth and gloss. A ceramic coating applied over those residues bonds to the oil layer, not to the paint. The coating still cures. It just cures on top of a layer that will eventually migrate, oxidize, or degrade from beneath.

Fingerprints, silicone transfer from trim dressings, and atmospheric contamination landing between the correction step and coating application compound the problem. In Florida, where ambient humidity runs high for most of the year and panels sitting outside accumulate surface film quickly, the window between completed prep and coating application is shorter than most guides assume. Working in Pasco County or North Hillsborough means dealing with airborne particulate and moisture redeposition on a timeline that makes immediate panel wiping non-negotiable.

The IPA wipe dissolves and lifts all of it in a single pass – when done correctly.

## Dilution

Isopropyl alcohol for panel wipe applications should be diluted to 70 to 90 percent. Straight 99 percent IPA evaporates too quickly in hot conditions, and Tampa Bay summer temperatures mean panel surface temperatures that accelerate evaporation well past what ambient air temperature suggests. At 99 percent, the carrier flashes before the solution has finished dissolving the residue layer, leaving partials behind.

At 70 percent, the water content extends dwell time slightly and helps carry dissolved residue off the surface rather than redistributing it. Below 70 percent, the water ratio becomes high enough that the solution deposits its own mineral trace as it evaporates on a warm panel.

Pre-diluted IPA in a labeled spray bottle, mixed fresh per session, is the correct approach. Do not use IPA stored in a partially open container – the alcohol content drifts over time as the more volatile component off-gases.

## Wipe Technique

The technique is where most panel wipe steps fail even when the product is correct.

Spray the IPA directly onto the towel, not onto the panel. Spraying directly onto a hot panel in direct sun replicates the evaporation problem regardless of dilution. A damp towel applied immediately after loading allows the solution to dwell under the towel face, which is where it needs to work.

Wipe in one direction. One pass per section. Do not circle back over an area already wiped – that redistributes dissolved residue onto clean paint rather than removing it. Work top to bottom, panel by panel, and rotate to a clean towel face before moving to the next section.

## After the Wipe

Once the IPA has fully evaporated – typically 60 to 90 seconds at ambient temperature, longer when humidity exceeds 75 percent – coating application begins immediately. Do not let the vehicle sit. Contamination redeposition starts the moment the panel is clean, and a wiped panel in Tampa Bay afternoon humidity accumulates atmospheric film faster than most owners expect.

If you reach the end of the panel wipe sequence and cannot begin coating application within a few minutes, repeat the wipe on any sections that have been waiting more than five minutes. The incremental product cost is negligible compared to a coating bonded to redeposited contamination.

The IPA step confirms surface readiness – it does not substitute for the steps that precede it. Paint correction, decontamination, and wash quality all feed into whether the wipe step has anything useful to work with.
